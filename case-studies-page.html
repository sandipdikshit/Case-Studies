0-TO-1 BUILD

Multi-Modal Agentic RCM Platform

Building an autonomous revenue cycle management system that reduced manual variance by 78% and improved collections by 34%

Company Input Zero Technologies
Timeline Apr 2024 - Present
Role Product Manager
Team Size 5-10 people

The Challenge

Healthcare providers were drowning in manual revenue cycle management work—verifying benefits, processing prior authorizations, and handling claims denials. The industry standard was 18% manual variance in RCM processes, leading to $2.1M+ in annual operational costs and delayed cash flow. Traditional RCM solutions were rule-based systems that couldn't adapt to the complexity of multi-payer environments.

Core Problem: How do we build an AI system that can autonomously handle the full RCM workflow while maintaining accuracy across different payer requirements and clinical contexts?

My Approach & Decision Framework

Product Strategy Framework
1
User Research Deep Dive
Conducted 25+ interviews with RCM specialists, identified top 3 pain points: benefits verification accuracy, prior auth bottlenecks, and denial management
2
Modular Architecture Decision
Chose multi-agentic system over monolithic approach—allows independent scaling and updates per workflow module
3
MVP Prioritization (RICE Framework)
Started with benefits verification agent (highest reach, highest impact, lowest effort). Validated with 5 pilot customers before building next agent
4
Confidence Threshold Tuning
Implemented variable confidence thresholds per payer—critical for multi-payer versatility while maintaining accuracy

Key Challenges & How I Overcame Them

  • Challenge: Clinical validation accuracy varied wildly across payers (55-85%)
    Solution: Built payer-specific training datasets and implemented adaptive confidence thresholds. Result: Achieved 70%+ consistent IPA across all payers
  • Challenge: Engineering team wanted to build everything at once, risking 6+ month launch delay
    Solution: Championed MVP-first approach—shipped benefits verification agent in 3 months, validated with real customers, then expanded. This reduced risk and provided early revenue
  • Challenge: Sales wanted to promise features we hadn't built yet to close deals
    Solution: Created clear product roadmap with T-shirt sizing and confidence levels. Established "committed" vs "planned" feature categories. This aligned expectations and prevented over-promising
  • Challenge: Value-based care models required different success metrics than traditional FFS
    Solution: Designed dual-metric system—tracked both traditional RCM metrics (collection rate, denial rate) and VBC metrics (quality measure impact, risk adjustment accuracy). Achieved 10% revenue lift in VBC models

Product Execution Timeline

Month 1-2: Discovery & Validation
User research, competitive analysis, technical feasibility assessment. Defined North Star metric: Manual Variance Reduction
Month 3-4: MVP Build - Benefits Verification Agent
Launched first autonomous agent with 5 pilot customers. Achieved 65% accuracy baseline
Month 5-7: Iteration & Prior Auth Agent
Improved BV agent to 70%+ accuracy. Launched PA agent. Manual variance dropped from 18% to 12%
Month 8-10: Scaling & Clinical IPA
Added clinical validation and denial management agents. Manual variance reached 4%. Collection rate improved 34%
Month 11-12: VBC Expansion
Launched value-based care optimization features. Achieved 10% revenue lift for VBC customers

What I Learned

  • Modular beats monolithic in AI systems: Building independent agents allowed us to iterate and improve each workflow without breaking others. This architectural decision was crucial for our velocity
  • Confidence thresholds are product decisions, not just ML decisions: We initially treated confidence scoring as purely technical. Realizing it was a product lever—tunable per payer and workflow—was a breakthrough
  • Early customer validation prevents wasted engineering: Our MVP approach with pilot customers saved us from building 3 features that users said they wanted but never used
  • Healthcare AI requires domain-specific success metrics: Traditional SaaS metrics weren't sufficient. We had to define new KPIs like "clinical accuracy per payer" and "autonomous resolution rate"
SCALING

AI-Powered Clinical Validation Platform

Scaling predictive models to 91% accuracy and driving $20M+ ARR with multi-payer support

Company Tab32
Timeline Jun 2022 - Apr 2024
Role AI Product Manager
Team Size 8-12 people

The Challenge

Tab32 had built a dental practice management platform with basic clinical validation, but accuracy was stuck at 78% and struggling with edge cases. The product was live with paying customers, but churn was increasing due to false positives in clinical alerts. We needed to improve accuracy without breaking existing integrations or disrupting the user experience for 500+ practices.

Core Problem: How do we improve model accuracy from 78% to 90%+ while maintaining backward compatibility, managing customer expectations, and scaling to support multiple insurance payers?

My Approach & Decision Framework

This was a scaling challenge, not a 0-to-1 build, which required different product thinking:

Scaling Strategy Framework
1
Root Cause Analysis
Analyzed 10,000+ false positives to identify patterns. Found 60% came from 3 edge cases in payer-specific rules
2
Phased Rollout Strategy
Created 3-tier rollout: shadow mode (collect data), opt-in beta (early adopters), full release. This de-risked the model updates
3
Multi-Payer Support Design
Built payer-agnostic core with payer-specific adapters. Enabled rapid expansion without rewriting core logic
4
Customer Communication Plan
Proactive communication with top 50 customers before each release. Set expectations on accuracy improvements and gathered feedback

Key Challenges & Solutions

  • Challenge: Model improvements caused UI behavior changes that confused existing users
    Solution: Built "explanation UI" showing why validation flagged each case. Reduced support tickets by 45% and improved user trust
  • Challenge: Data science team wanted to rebuild entire model from scratch (6 month effort)
    Solution: Advocated for incremental improvements approach—targeted fixes for top 3 error patterns. Achieved 91% accuracy in 3 months vs 6+ month rebuild
  • Challenge: Adding new payers required 2-3 weeks of custom engineering each time
    Solution: Refactored architecture to payer-agnostic core with config-driven adapters. Reduced new payer onboarding to 2-3 days
  • Challenge: Enterprise customers wanted custom validation rules but this would fragment the product
    Solution: Built rule configuration UI with guardrails—customers could tune sensitivity but not fundamentally change validation logic. Satisfied 90% of customization requests without technical debt

What I Learned

  • Incremental improvement beats rebuild in most cases: The data science team wanted a ground-up rebuild. By focusing on highest-impact error patterns, we achieved 91% accuracy in half the time
  • Explanation UI is critical for AI products: Users don't just want accurate results—they want to understand WHY the AI made a decision. This builds trust and reduces support burden
  • Architecture enables velocity: The payer-agnostic design decision paid dividends. We went from supporting 3 payers to 15 payers without scaling engineering team
  • Customer communication prevents churn during product changes: Proactive communication about model updates—what's changing, why, and what to expect—prevented churn during a sensitive transition period
GROWTH & EXPANSION

UK Practice Management Platform Expansion

Scaling operations from £0 to £3.4B managed across 500+ healthcare practices

Company AltrisDev UK
Timeline Apr 2022 - Jun 2022
Role Product Manager
Team Size 12-15 people

The Challenge

AltrisDev had a successful US-based practice management platform but wanted to expand to the UK market—a completely different regulatory environment (NHS vs US insurance), different clinical workflows, and different user expectations. The challenge wasn't just localization—it required rethinking core product assumptions while maintaining the codebase shared with the US product.

Core Problem: How do we expand to a new market with fundamentally different requirements while maintaining a single codebase, meeting aggressive growth targets, and building trust with a new user base?

My Approach & Decision Framework

Market Expansion Strategy
1
Market Research & Localization Needs
Conducted 40+ interviews with UK healthcare providers. Identified 8 critical differences from US market in workflows and expectations
2
Build vs Buy vs Partner Decision
Analyzed options for NHS integration. Chose partnership with existing NHS connector vs building from scratch—saved 4 months
3
Cloud-Native Architecture Migration
Made strategic decision to rebuild UK deployment on cloud-native stack. Enabled faster iteration and better compliance with UK data regulations
4
Dashboard-First Strategy
Built 500+ practice-specific dashboards before launching full platform. Proved value quickly and gathered usage data to inform roadmap

Key Challenges & Solutions

  • Challenge: UK data regulations (GDPR) were stricter than US, requiring major architecture changes
    Solution: Advocated for UK-specific data residency and implemented data minimization patterns. This became a competitive advantage vs US competitors
  • Challenge: Engineering team wanted to fork codebase for UK, which would create long-term maintenance nightmare
    Solution: Pushed for configuration-driven approach with feature flags and regional adapters. Maintained single codebase while supporting market-specific features
  • Challenge: NHS integration was complex and poorly documented
    Solution: Partnered with UK-based NHS integration specialist rather than building ourselves. Strategic partnership vs build decision saved 4+ months
  • Challenge: UK users expected different UX patterns than US users (more data density, different navigation)
    Solution: Built configurable UI framework that allowed region-specific layouts without forking UI code. Satisfied UK preferences while maintaining US experience

What I Learned

  • Market expansion is product strategy, not just localization: Simply translating the US product wouldn't have worked. Understanding UK healthcare workflows required rethinking core assumptions
  • Partner vs build decisions accelerate growth: The NHS integration partnership was critical. Would have taken 6+ months to build ourselves, and partners had domain expertise we lacked
  • Single codebase with configuration beats forking: Fighting to maintain one codebase was worth it. By month 6, features built for UK were being adopted by US customers
  • Dashboard-first strategy proves value quickly: Instead of launching full platform, we led with dashboards. Practices saw value in week 1, which built trust for deeper integration
