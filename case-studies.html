The Problem No One Had Really Solved

US mid-tier healthcare providers (50–200 clinicians) were operating in a broken revenue cycle. The largest players had enterprise RCM vendors. Small practices used billing agencies. But the mid-tier — 200+ provider groups processing $100M+ in annual claims — were stuck with fragmented point solutions that couldn't talk to each other.

The Core Tension Every claim touches 8+ systems before cash hits the bank account: EHR → coding → eligibility → prior auth → submission → tracking → denial management → collections. Each system was a different vendor, a different team, a different SLA. The leakage at each handoff was the problem.

Industry average clean claim rate was 81.3%. That means 1 in 5 claims required rework. Each rework cost $25–$118 in administrative labor. At $120M monthly claim volume, that's $1.8M–$8.5M in waste per year — before you count denied revenue that was never recovered.

How I Approached It

Starting from zero — no product team, no established process, no defined roadmap — the first 45 days were about discovery before any line of code was written.

1
Workflow Archaeology (Days 1–30)
Shadowed RCM specialists across 8 provider sites. Mapped every step in the claim lifecycle with timestamps. Found that 73% of denial-causing errors were created upstream at eligibility verification — before the claim was even submitted.
Key insight: Fix eligibility, and denials fall by 60%+ downstream
2
Architecture Decision: Multi-Agent vs. Monolith
Engineering team advocated for a single unified model. I pushed for a modular multi-agent architecture. The tradeoff: higher upfront complexity, but each agent (eligibility, prior auth, coding, denial) could be independently retrained when payer rules changed — which they do every 90 days.
Decision: Multi-agent. Paid off when we onboarded 3 new payers in 2 weeks without touching other modules
3
MVP Prioritization via RICE + Revenue Impact
Scored 14 potential modules across Reach, Impact, Confidence, Effort — then overlaid financial model to prioritize by ARR protection potential. Benefits verification agent won: highest volume, highest error rate, shortest feedback loop for ML training.
First pilot: 5 clients, 30 days. Clean claim rate improved from 81% → 91%
4
The Ambient Documentation Bet (Neoscribe)
Discovered physicians were spending 2–3 hrs/day on documentation post-encounter. This was upstream of RCM — bad documentation caused coding errors which caused denials. Built Neoscribe as a separate module that sits before the RCM cycle, not inside it. This created a defensible distribution wedge: once a provider uses ambient documentation, switching costs are enormous.
15K+ daily encounters processed · 95% SOAP note accuracy · 140% ARR growth
5
Scaling the Team: 0 → 25 Without Burning Budget
Built the product department with 40% lower burn rate vs industry average. Key decisions: hired engineering managers over engineers (force multipliers), embedded solution architects with clients (feedback velocity), and ran bi-weekly release cycles using RAD framework that cut time-to-market by 40%.

What Made This Different

Most RCM vendors record what happened. This platform prevented it from happening — 48–72 hours before submission.

⚡
Autonomous Payer Routing — Not Rule-Based
Instead of static payer rules, the routing engine uses a real-time ensemble model trained on 90-day rolling payer approval rates by CPT code, ZIP, and facility. When Cigna's denial rate for spinal fusion spiked at a specific ZIP, the engine rerouted to Aetna automatically — saving $22,400 per affected claim.
vs. Competitors: Change Healthcare, Availity → static rule trees that require manual updates per payer contract
🧠
Multi-Pass Clinical Validation with Confidence Scoring
Every AI-generated clinical statement carries a confidence threshold. Statements below 90% confidence are flagged for physician review. This eliminates the hallucination risk that plagues basic scribes — documented in NPJ Digital Medicine (2025). Result: 95% accurate SOAP notes with zero fabricated clinical findings.
vs. Competitors: Nuance DAX, Abridge → generate documentation without per-statement confidence; hallucination rate 1–3% at scale = clinical risk
🔄
Self-Healing Revenue Cycle
When a denial pattern is detected, the system doesn't just fix the current claim — it traces the error to its upstream origin (wrong modifier, missing eligibility step, EHR field) and auto-corrects the template for future encounters. Denial growth reduced 30% MoM through root-cause elimination.
vs. Competitors: Athenahealth, Epic RCM → fix symptoms per claim; no upstream correction; denial patterns recur monthly
🏦
Modular Architecture with Consumption-Based Pricing
Competitors price on per-provider seat. We priced on claims processed — aligning incentives with outcomes. A provider paying $0 in months with fewer claims but scaling with volume makes the product sticky through growth rather than sticky through contracts.
vs. Competitors: Kareo, AdvancedMD → per-seat licensing that penalizes growth and doesn't align with provider cash flow cycles

What I Actually Learned

01
The upstream fix is almost always more valuable than the downstream patch
Every RCM vendor sold denial management. We won by fixing documentation before it became a denial. 73% of denials traced to eligibility and coding errors created 2 steps earlier. Going upstream 48 hours eliminated the downstream firefighting.
02
Modular architecture is a business strategy, not just a technical choice
The decision to build multi-agent rather than monolith paid off when payer rules changed (every 90 days). We could retrain one agent without touching others. Competitors with monolithic models had 3-month update cycles. We had 2-week cycles.
03
Consumption pricing aligned incentives in ways seat licensing never could
When providers grew, they paid more — and were delighted to, because the value was proportional. Net revenue retention hit 150% because growth meant more claims, not more seats to negotiate.
04
Explanation UI is the trust layer — not a nice-to-have
Physicians rejected AI output they couldn't interrogate. When we added per-statement confidence scores and source citations to every SOAP note, adoption went from 34% to 60% within 6 weeks. The AI was the same. The explanation was new.

The Problem: CPG Procurement Was Flying Blind

Reckitt, ABInBev, and their peer CPG companies were making commodity hedging decisions — covering millions in NFDM, Ethylene, Palm Oil, and Propylene — using a combination of Excel, agency forecasts from Mintec/USDA, and quarterly CRM meetings. The output was a static coverage recommendation based on rules that hadn't changed in years.

The Legacy CNC Tool's Fundamental Flaw The parameterized rule-based CNC system worked by backtesting 340+ parameter combinations historically and selecting the set with the best M2M performance. Problem: it assumed the future looked like the past, had no signal fusion (just two inputs), and required a human expert to interpret a table of 340 rows every quarter. It was a decision support tool pretending to be intelligence.

How I Approached It

Rethinking What "Hedging Intelligence" Actually Means

Decision: Build a five-layer intelligence stack, not a recommendation calculator

Built a signal fusion layer that weighted each source by its historical predictive accuracy for each commodity.

Credit Risk as a One-Time Score Is Broken

Every lender in India runs a CIBIL check at underwriting. The credit score is valid at the moment of origination. By the time a borrower becomes 30-day delinquent, the lender has already lost the window to intervene — collection costs spike, recovery rates fall, and the NPL ends up in the provision bucket.

The Core Flaw Credit scoring is a snapshot. Creditworthiness is a trajectory. A borrower with a 750 CIBIL score who just lost their primary business contract is a worse credit risk than a 680-score borrower whose revenue has grown 40% in 90 days. The score doesn't know. The lender doesn't know. The default happens anyway.

My Approach

1
4-State Markov Model as the Core Primitive
Instead of binary (good/bad) credit scoring, modeled borrowers as transitioning between Healthy → Tight → Strained → Distressed states with a Markov transition matrix. This gives: (a) state probability at any point in time, (b) velocity — how fast they're moving between states, and (c) lead time before delinquency — the intervention window.
Distress detected at T–21 days with 82%+ probability at the right confidence band
2
Behavioral Deviation + Velocity as Leading Indicators
Traditional models use lag indicators (missed payment, DSO change). Built the velocity index as a leading indicator: rate of change in transaction patterns, bureau inquiry frequency, and repayment behavioral deviation from the borrower's own historical baseline — not just peer comparison.
Velocity index 0.91 = HIGH signal 3 weeks before any payment is missed
3
Elasticity Engine — Safe Credit Headroom
Counterintuitively, the platform also identifies upside: borrowers whose creditworthiness has improved post-origination but whose credit limit hasn't been refreshed. The Elasticity Engine surfaces ₹47Cr in safe additional capacity across 4,800+ borrowers — a revenue opportunity the lender was leaving on the table.
₹47Cr additional safe credit capacity identified · Revenue upside, not just risk mitigation

What's Different About CreditOS

📡
Continuous Monitoring vs. Point-in-Time Scoring
Every traditional credit model scores at origination and at scheduled review (quarterly/annual). CreditOS runs continuously — monitoring all 4,800+ borrowers in real time. The state of every borrower is always current, not stale.
vs. CIBIL / TransUnion / Experian → bureau scores update monthly at best; no behavioral trajectory tracking
🔀
Markov State Transition — Trajectory, Not Snapshot
The 4-state Markov matrix computes the probability of transitioning from Tight to Strained within 30 days — before either state has materialized in payment behavior. This is the distinction between a risk measurement tool and a risk prediction tool.
vs. Most NBFC risk systems → rules-based bucket assignment; no transition probability computation; no velocity concept
🎯
Bias Monitor — Responsible AI Layer
The platform monitors its own models for demographic bias. When CRED-v2.1 flagged a gender feature with disproportionate impact in feature importance analysis, the bias monitor surfaced it to the risk team before deployment. Regulators increasingly require this; CreditOS provides it as a native capability.
vs. Internal risk models at most lenders → no explainability layer; no bias testing; audit trail is manual

The Biosimilar Launch Problem

Aimovig (erenumab) was Amgen's first-to-market CGRP inhibitor for migraine. With biosimilar entrants approaching across multiple geographies, Amgen needed to understand provider-level switching intent, formulary access barriers, and optimal target segments before competitors captured share.

The Insight Gap Amgen's US commercial team had claims data and prescriber lists. What they didn't have: a forward-looking model of which physicians were most likely to switch to a biosimilar, why, and what interventions would change that probability. The difference between a list of targets and a predictive targeting engine was the $45M opportunity.

Across 7 countries, the problem was more acute: different formulary structures, different payer systems, different patient pathways. A US launch playbook couldn't be templated. Each market needed a localized simulation with shared underlying methodology.

My Approach

1
Provider Survey × Predictive Modeling Hybrid
Surveyed 270 neurologists and headache specialists. This wasn't market research — it was training data. Combined survey responses with prescribing claims data to build predictive share models that mapped provider attitudes to behavioral proxies we could track in claims data going forward.
91% accuracy vs actual launch performance · $200M+ investment decisions influenced
2
Market Simulation Platform for 7-Country Launch
Built a simulation platform that modeled biosimilar penetration scenarios under different pricing, access, and messaging assumptions. Each country had a calibrated model using local payer data, formulary access tiers, and patient journey analysis. Allowed Amgen's regional teams to run "what if" scenarios before committing resources.
23% higher peak share projected vs. benchmark launches · 6 months time-to-market reduction
3
Provider Targeting Engine — Turning Insight into Action
The final output wasn't a slide deck. It was a provider targeting engine that ranked every physician by: switching probability, potential scripts per year, access pathway, and intervention type most likely to change behavior. The commercial team could filter by territory, specialty, and priority band and get call-ready intelligence.
Actionable at the rep level — not just at the strategy level
